
George Orwell was an English novelist, essayist, journalist, and critic.
He is best known for his novels '1984' and 'Animal Farm', which are critical of totalitarian regimes.
Orwell's work is characterized by lucid prose, social criticism, opposition to totalitarianism, and support of democratic socialism.
He was born Eric Arthur Blair in 1903 in Motihari, India, and died in 1950 in London, England.
Orwell's writing often explored themes of social injustice, political oppression, and the misuse of language.
His father was a minor official in the Opium Department of the Indian Civil Service.
Orwell only lived in India for a little over a year before moving back to England.
His family was part of the "landless gentry" class in India.
He rarely wrote about India itself as a child's experience.
Orwell later served as an Imperial Police officer in Burma which was then part of British India.
He spent five unhappy years in Burma, a period that heavily influenced his anti-imperialist views.
Orwell "hated the imperialism" he was serving in India and Burma with a bitterness he struggled to adequately express.
He felt an "immense weight of guilt" over his role in the British Empire.
He sought to expiate his guilt through his later writing and political stances.
Orwell argued that one could only truly hate imperialism by being part of its oppressive "machinery of despotism".
He experienced mutual distrust between colonizers and the colonized while in Burma.
Orwell feared ridicule and humiliation by the "natives" more than physical danger.
This fear is a central theme in his essay "Shooting an Elephant".
Orwell believed that when "the white man turns tyrant it is his own freedom that he destroys" in the act of maintaining power over natives.
He expressed contempt for Indian nationalism in his essay "Notes on Nationalism".
Orwell viewed Indian nationalism as part of the "lunatic modern habit of identifying oneself with large power units".
He linked Indian nationalism with other forms of extreme patriotism which he disliked.
Orwell did not have much enthusiasm for the Indian National Congress leaders.
In his proposals for English socialism, Orwell advocated for "Immediate Dominion status for India, with power to secede when the war is over".
Orwell argued that Indian independence was a lost cause if fascist nations were allowed to dominate the world.
He noted that the British establishment often viewed Gandhi as "our man" because his non-violence prevented effective armed conflict.
In his essay "Reflections on Gandhi", Orwell noted the success of pacifism against the British administration.
Orwell wondered if pacifist methods would be effective against totalitarian regimes like Hitler's or Stalin's.
He believed the British in India used a form of soft power and hypocrisy rather than the overt brutality of totalitarian regimes.
Orwell worked for the BBC's Eastern Service during WWII, creating propaganda broadcasts for India.
He found the work of broadcasting British propaganda to India to be a "hopeless task".
Orwell resigned from the BBC because he felt the Indian audience was largely uninterested in their broadcasts.
He believed his audience in India mostly ignored the propaganda.
Orwell criticized some British officials and their "pukka sahib" attitudes toward Indians.
He felt that the British ruling class in India was narrow-minded and detached.
Orwell observed a stark social divide between the British rulers and the Indian people.
He had a deep empathy for the plight of the common, colonized people in general.
Orwell did not write extensively about Indian culture itself, focusing more on the political relationship.
He saw British rule in India as inherently oppressive and morally indefensible.
Orwell believed that the empire corrupted the souls of both the rulers and the ruled.
He saw the Indian people as subjects whose liberty was being denied by a foreign power.
